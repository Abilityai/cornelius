# The revolving door - a pattern that implicates the leader not the hires

## Core Framework

One of the most diagnostically significant patterns at the Scale threshold is what the document calls "the revolving door": COOs, heads of sales, and other senior hires brought in as saviours and dismissed within months. The pattern repeats. Each cycle is blamed on the individual. The leader rarely sees their own role in it.

This pattern is significant precisely because of its repetition. A single failed executive hire might be bad luck or poor selection. A recurring pattern - particularly at the same level of seniority and with the same ending - is a system dynamic, not a hiring problem. It implicates the conditions the leader is creating, not the calibre of the people being hired.

---

## Context and Acceptance

**Field Acceptance**: Recognised in systems-thinking approaches to leadership; less visible in organisations where the CEO narrative dominates
**Supporting Evidence**: Consistent with Senge's systems thinking, Argyris's double-loop learning, and Kegan's constructive-developmental theory
**Alternative Framing**: Most organisations diagnose this as a talent pipeline or culture-fit problem - a diagnosis that perpetuates rather than resolves the cycle

---

## Why the Pattern Persists

The leader at this threshold typically externalises the problem first - "we haven't found the right person yet." This is the most natural and least threatening interpretation. It is also, almost always, incomplete.

What the pattern usually reveals, when examined closely:
- The leader continues to make decisions that should belong to the new hire, undermining their authority
- The implicit brief given to the hire is impossible: take ownership, but don't actually change anything
- The leader experiences the new person's different approach as threat, not contribution
- When the relationship fractures, the exit is attributed to performance, not dynamic

The moment of recognition - when the leader begins to see their own place in the pattern - is described in Luminous coaching practice as one of the most consequential turning points at this threshold. It requires the leader to hold two difficult truths simultaneously: that they are both deeply committed to the organisation's success and one of its current constraints.
