# "Legacy" Is a Problematic Word - Language Intentionality Across Gender and Culture

## Core Framework

The word "legacy" carries significant cultural and gendered baggage that makes it land poorly with meaningful portions of the audience Luminous serves. It evokes a masculine, hierarchical, monument-building sensibility - plaques, buildings, institutions named after the benefactor. It skews old (associated with the final chapter of a career or life, not its active middle). And it can feel self-aggrandising in cultures where humility is expected.

The practitioner observation: "long-term impact," "stewardship," or "what you're leaving behind" land better across genders and cultures. Each carries the same underlying idea with a different emotional register.

**Preferred language**:
- "Long-term impact" - forward-looking, active, systemic
- "Stewardship" - relational, humble, custodial
- "What you're leaving behind" - plain, honest, accessible
- "What remains" (the Luminous brand name) - poetic, open, non-hierarchical

**Language to use carefully or contextually**:
- "Legacy" - can work with certain audiences (typically older male leaders in Western contexts who have already claimed the word as theirs), but should not be the default
- "Heritage" - similar risks

## Context & Acceptance

**Field Acceptance**: Emerging - this is a practitioner observation grounded in coaching experience rather than empirical research. It is consistent with sociolinguistic research on gendered language in professional contexts and with insights from cross-cultural communication.

**Why This Matters Beyond Style**: Language is not merely cosmetic at this threshold. If the word "legacy" creates resistance or irrelevance - however subtle - the leader may dismiss the entire territory of the work before they have entered it. The wrong word closes the door.

**The Broader Principle**: Every word at the Long-Term Impact threshold should be audited for: (a) gendered connotations, (b) age/stage associations, (c) cultural resonance across geographies. The content of the work is universal. The language of access is not.

## Connection to the "Not Just for Those Winding Down" Observation

A related insight from the same document: this threshold is live for a 40-year-old founder as much as a 65-year-old retiring CEO. The framing - including word choice - should not skew old or it will feel irrelevant to a large part of the audience. "Legacy" is particularly prone to this: it implies a body of work already complete, being handed forward. "Long-term impact" can describe a 40-year-old's intention as naturally as a 65-year-old's reflection.
