## Core Framework

A distinctive feature of family business succession - particularly in multi-heir situations - is that the conventional corporate solution (elect a single capable leader) is often unavailable or inadequate. When multiple siblings, cousins, or extended family members hold co-ownership or co-governance stakes, decision-making authority becomes murky, contested, and structurally ambiguous. This is the governance gap: the space between who legally owns the enterprise and who has the clarity and legitimacy to lead it.

## What Makes This Hard

**Structural complexity:**
- Multiple legitimate claimants to authority across ownership, management, and family dimensions
- Non-family senior executives with institutional knowledge and informal power
- Competing generational expectations about how the business should be governed

**Relational complexity:**
- Sibling rivalry and historical patterns of alliance and exclusion
- "Unholy alliances" - cross-generational coalitions that protect particular interests
- The sidelining of weaker performers who still carry ownership stakes

**Trust barriers:**
- Family businesses are often reluctant to open their books or admit external help
- This makes it difficult to access the information needed to design effective governance structures

---

## The Governance Design Challenge

The task is not simply choosing a successor - it is designing a governance architecture that:
1. Distributes authority clearly across ownership, management, and family spheres
2. Creates legitimate mechanisms for collective decision-making when no single heir can be named CEO
3. Protects the business from becoming a theatre for unresolved family conflict

This requires combining legal/structural expertise with the relational and systemic work that Luminous specialises in.
