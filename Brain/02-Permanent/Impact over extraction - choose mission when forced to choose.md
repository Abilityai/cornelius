# Impact over extraction - choose mission when forced to choose

The Canon is careful not to be anti-wealth. It welcomes wealth creation explicitly - as energy that enables depth, supports well-being, and funds independence over the long term. This is an important nuance. Luminous is not built on a romantic suspicion of financial success. It understands that an under-resourced institution cannot serve its mission at the level it aspires to.

But the Canon is equally clear about what happens when these two goods - financial growth and mission impact - come into genuine conflict. In that moment, Luminous chooses impact. This is not a platitude. It has teeth because it is one of the Seven Seals: structurally protected, enforced by the Foundation's oversight, and not subject to revision by any individual leader or generation of partners.

The practical bite of this principle lies in the prohibition on debt that compromises institutional independence. Many organisations that begin with noble missions slowly compromise them under the logic of financial necessity - the funding that comes with strings, the partnership that requires softening inconvenient truths, the growth strategy that slowly shifts focus towards more lucrative but less impactful clients. Each individual step seems reasonable. The accumulated drift is devastating.

By naming this principle as a Seal, Luminous commits in advance to refusing these incremental compromises. The institution exists to serve its purpose. Extraction - taking more value than you create or taking value that properly belongs to the mission - is positioned not merely as strategically unwise but as a violation of the institution's identity.
