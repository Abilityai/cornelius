# Vow of Stewardship - act for a future you will not see

The Third Vow reframes the relationship between a practitioner and the institution. Luminous is not something you own, lead, or use - it is something you hold in trust. This distinction has profound practical implications. An owner maximises value for themselves. A steward maximises value for the institution and those it serves, including those who will come after.

"This institution existed before me and must endure beyond me." This is the essential psychological reorientation the vow requires. Whatever role a practitioner holds - founding partner, senior practitioner, programme director - the institution's story is longer than their chapter in it. They inherit something, they contribute to it, and they pass it on. The quality of what they pass on is their legacy, more than any individual achievement during their tenure.

"Act as a steward for a future you will not see" is one of the more demanding phrases in the Vows. It asks practitioners to genuinely orient their decisions towards outcomes they will not personally experience. This runs against most of the incentive structures in professional life, which reward visible results within manageable timescales. Taking this seriously means considering how decisions made today will look in fifty years - in terms of the institution's health, its reputation, the quality of the practitioners it has developed, and the fidelity of the work to its original purpose.

"Release authority when your time has passed" is the final movement of the vow, and perhaps the most personally demanding. The willingness to step back, to hand over, to support one's successor without qualification - this is where stewardship is tested. The vow commits practitioners to this release before the moment arrives.
