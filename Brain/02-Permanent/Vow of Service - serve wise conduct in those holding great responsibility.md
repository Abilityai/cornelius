# Vow of Service - serve wise conduct in those holding great responsibility

The First Vow names the work precisely: not service to leaders as people, not service to their organisations, and not service to their success as they define it. Service to wise conduct. This is a subtle but important distinction. A practitioner who simply helps a leader achieve their goals is a consultant or a coach. A practitioner who has taken this vow is something different - someone whose service is oriented towards the quality of judgment and conscience that the leader brings to their decisions.

The temporal frame of the vow is striking: "decisions made today honour the dignity of all living beings and the wellbeing of generations yet unborn." This is not the usual frame of executive coaching, which tends to focus on the leader's performance, the organisation's results, or the next quarter. The First Vow places the practitioner's work in a chain of consequence that extends far beyond the present moment. The leader being served is a link in a much longer chain.

"Serve responsibility guided by conscience" is the vow's closing commitment. It positions conscience - not client satisfaction, not business outcomes, not measurable impact metrics - as the ultimate guide to what the work is for. A practitioner who has taken this vow is obligated to serve in ways that may occasionally be uncomfortable for the leader being served: raising what is being avoided, naming what is true but unwelcome, declining to help with what is expedient but not wise.

The First Vow is the foundation on which all the others rest. Without it, the subsequent vows are professional commitments. With it, they become part of a way of being in service.
