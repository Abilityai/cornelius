# Vow of Integrity - choose truth over growth

The Second Vow asks practitioners to act with integrity "even when it is costly." This qualifier is doing important work. Integrity that costs nothing is not really integrity - it is comfort. The vow is specifically about the moments when truth and expedience diverge, when choosing what is right means accepting a real loss. In those moments, the vow provides orientation: choose truth.

The specific framing - choosing what is right over what is expedient - points to one of the most common forms of institutional and personal compromise. Expedient decisions look justifiable in the short term; they solve immediate problems and satisfy immediate pressures. But they accumulate. An institution or a practitioner who consistently chooses expedience builds a reputation for reliability in the wrong direction: reliable to bend when the pressure is sufficient, reliable to prioritise growth over principle when forced to choose.

The protection of "the reputation and moral centre of Luminous above personal gain" is a notable commitment. It asks practitioners to understand their personal conduct as carrying institutional meaning. This is a heavier obligation than simply being honest in one's own dealings. It means that a practitioner's choices - particularly the difficult ones, the ones made in private or under pressure - either strengthen or weaken the institution's integrity as a whole.

The final sentence of the vow is clean and absolute: "when faced with a choice between growth and truth, I vow to choose truth." There is no qualification here, no appeal to context or circumstance. This is the kind of commitment that actually binds - because it is stated in advance, before the moment of temptation arrives.
