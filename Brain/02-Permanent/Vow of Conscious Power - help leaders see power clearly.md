# Vow of Conscious Power - help leaders see power clearly

The Fourth Vow does not ask practitioners to hold power for leaders or to tell leaders what to do with their power. It asks them to help leaders see power clearly - "its weight, its consequences, and its moral reach." This is a specific and demanding task. Most leaders are not lacking information about their power. What they often lack is the capacity to be fully present to what it actually means: the scale of what their decisions affect, the invisibility of consequences that unfold at a distance, the moral weight of choices that seem merely technical.

The phrase "moral reach" is particularly evocative. Power extends further than its exercise is visible. A decision about supply chains has moral reach into the lives of workers the leader will never meet. A policy choice has moral reach into communities not represented in the room. A financial allocation has moral reach into futures not yet lived. Helping a leader see this reach - without paralyzing them with it, but also without allowing them to pretend it does not exist - is the practitioner's core task under this vow.

The vow then specifies the manner of guidance: "with wisdom, courage, and skilful compassion, always honouring their autonomy and agency." This last clause is essential. The practitioner is not there to replace the leader's judgment with their own. They are not there to redirect the leader towards conclusions the practitioner has already reached. The work is to expand the leader's field of vision so that their own judgment - their own conscience - can operate more fully. This is the difference between coaching and manipulation.

Wisdom, courage, and skilful compassion are the practitioner's instruments. Each is necessary; none is sufficient alone.
