# Vow of Lineage - protect the canon while updating methods

The Sixth Vow holds a tension that is central to the survival of any living tradition: how do you honour what has been passed down while remaining responsive to what is genuinely new? The vow names this tension explicitly and offers a principle for navigating it - protect what must endure, update what must evolve.

The traditions honoured by the vow span the full range of humanity's accumulated wisdom: "Eastern and Western, ancient and emerging, spiritual and scientific." This breadth is not syncretic eclecticism - it is a statement that the sources of genuine wisdom are distributed, that no single tradition holds the complete understanding, and that the work of Luminous draws from many streams while serving one purpose. Honouring these traditions means remaining in active, respectful dialogue with them - not mining them for techniques but treating them as living transmissions that require proper relationship.

The commitment to protecting "the canon, rituals, and living knowledge of Luminous" is a specific obligation to the institution itself. Practitioners are not free to informally modify the canon to suit their preferences or their clients' tastes. The teachings have been shaped through long refinement and belong to the lineage, not to the individual practitioner. This requires a particular kind of humility - understanding oneself as a vehicle for something larger, not an author of it.

At the same time, "updating methods where needed" is an equally important commitment. A tradition that cannot adapt its methods becomes brittle - it preserves the form while losing the living quality. The canon's principles endure; the specific practices through which they are transmitted must be continuously renewed. The Sixth Vow asks practitioners to hold both obligations at once.
