# Ownership protection prevents capture by any single entity

The history of mission-driven organisations is littered with examples of institutional capture - where a founding vision is gradually or suddenly redirected to serve the interests of a dominant shareholder, a powerful funder, or a charismatic leader who conflates themselves with the institution. Luminous's second Seal is a structural defence against precisely this pattern.

The rule is precise: active partners and the Foundation must collectively hold at least 81% of Luminous at all times. No single external entity may hold more than 19%. This is not a guideline or an aspiration - it is a constitutional constraint. Combined with the Foundation's golden share, which carries super-voting rights on existential decisions, this creates a robust barrier against the most common forms of institutional capture.

The golden share is particularly important. It means that even in scenarios where commercial pressures are extreme - a financial crisis, a buyout offer that appears irresistibly generous, a moment where the pragmatic argument for compromise seems overwhelming - the Foundation can hold the line. The stewardship arm retains decisive power over any proposed sale or transfer of control, any amendment to the mission or vision, any change to the Seals themselves, and any dissolution of the institution.

This structure trusts the long game over the short game. It accepts that Luminous may sometimes forgo significant financial value in order to preserve institutional integrity. This is not naive - it is a deliberate choice about what the institution is actually for.
