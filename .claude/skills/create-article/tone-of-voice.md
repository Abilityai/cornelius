# Eugene Kurogo - Long-Form Article Tone of Voice

## Voice DNA (Primary Characteristics)

### Academic Authority with Accessibility (35%)
- Presents complex research and theory through digestible frameworks while maintaining intellectual rigor
- Examples:
  - "Noise is unwanted variability in judgments and decisions, especially repeated decisions. It is a measure of consistency in your decisions."
  - "If you wander around and find something you like (food, drug, behavior), then try it and like it, it triggers the release of Dopamine. This is an unexpected reward."
  - "Research from Sun Yat-sen University and Carnegie Mellon University confirms what many have suspected"

### Evidence-Based Provocateur (25%)
- Challenges conventional thinking with hard data and research, not just opinion
- Examples:
  - "The model of you beats you! Once again, the model trained on your judgments and predictions tends to do better than you on future decisions."
  - "I've got bad news for you - we're all enslaved. Most of us (myself included) don't have much control over what we want and do."
  - "In some studies, linear random models have outperformed humans..."

### Methodical Educator (20%)
- Systematically builds understanding through structured learning progressions
- Examples:
  - "You will learn: Why you should always get a second opinion... Why some court decisions... Why AI consistently..."
  - "To start, you need to make a number of assumptions, such as: The projected level of spending... ACV... Churn Rate..."
  - "Let's take a look: Focus on the Process, not the outcome... The psychological bias checklist..."

### Research-Driven Storyteller (15%)
- Uses empirical examples and case studies to illustrate abstract concepts
- Examples:
  - "A 1987 study showed that cats that fall from the 6th floor or higher are more likely to survive"
  - "Back in World War II, Americans were trying to make their planes more robust..."
  - "That's exactly what happened in the experiment with genetically modified mice..."

### Pragmatic Tool-Builder (5%)
- Creates actionable frameworks and tools from theoretical insights
- Examples:
  - "I've created a quick example of this formula in Google Sheets. Feel free to use it"
  - "So, to help founders I advise, I've developed a SaaS Model that allows you to..."

## Do's and Don'ts

### DO
- Open with clear learning objectives or what readers will discover
- Structure content with clear sections and subheadings
- Cite specific research, studies, and data points with precision
- Use numbered lists and bullet points for complex information
- Include TL;DR or summary sections for key takeaways
- Define technical terms immediately after introducing them
- Build arguments systematically from evidence to conclusion
- Provide tools, models, or frameworks readers can apply
- Use hyphens (-) instead of em-dashes (—)

### DON'T
- Never make claims without supporting evidence or research
- Avoid emotional appeals without data backing
- Don't use jargon without clear explanations
- Never hedge with "maybe," "perhaps," "it seems"
- Avoid passive voice ("it has been observed that...")
- Don't use corporate buzzwords ("synergy", "leverage", "utilize")
- Never be preachy or condescending
- Don't over-explain simple concepts
