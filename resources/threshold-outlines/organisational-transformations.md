## 1. Description of this threshold

- Arises when the culture of an organisation needs to shift - to respond to a new strategy, a changed world, or a leader who recognises that how things are being done is no longer fit for purpose
- We address the cultural aspect: how people show up: how they speak to each other, make decisions, hold power, and relate. There may also be more "technical" changes to processes, systems, or structures - however, we are not focused on this aspect of change implementation
- The starting point is always the leader - their behaviours, their patterns, their willingness to go first. Culture change that doesn't begin here tends to exhaust itself within a year or two and fade
- The goal is not to install a defined "new culture." It is to create the conditions in which transformation can occur - and then trust what emerges

## 2. Typical client profile

- A CEO or senior leader who senses that the way the organisation operates is holding it back - but may not yet see their own role in that
- Moments of genuine complexity: post-merger, rapid growth, external disruption, or a fundamental strategic pivot that the culture hasn't caught up with
- Also: leaders who want to move from a command-and-control style to one that is more empowering - but haven't yet understood what that actually requires of them personally

## 3. Common signals and challenges

- The cascade that goes nowhere - top-down culture programmes that generate workshops and fatigue, but no lasting shift; people disengage after 12-18 months
- Short-termism creating a vicious cycle - external pressure drives short-term focus, which creates anxiety, which reduces psychological safety, which makes transformation even harder
- Abdication dressed as empowerment - the leader says "you're all empowered, go forth" without providing direction, safety or genuine authority. People feel lost, not free
- Incoherence - people aren't speaking the truth. There is a gap between what is said in meetings and what is said in corridors. The organisation cannot transform what it cannot name
- Transformation as a project, not a way of being - organisations that treat culture change as a programme with a start and end date, rather than an ongoing commitment to leading differently

## 4. The opportunity for growth

- **For leaders:** to become genuinely curious rather than defended; to move from power-over to power-with; to discover that vulnerability and honesty are not weaknesses but the conditions for trust
- **For teams:** when leaders go first - naming what they don't know, acknowledging what isn't working, asking rather than telling - it gives permission for others to do the same. This is how cultures actually shift
- **For the organisation:**
  - From motive momentum to a movement - when enough people are working in a new way, and those people become ambassadors for others, change compounds and becomes self-sustaining
  - Moving from "cascading" to "waves" - where change spreads through warming, inspiring and involving rather than being pushed top-down. Waves go out through the organisation and return, and crucially, what comes back is carried forward in the next wave. Unlike a cascade, which hits the bottom and stops, this is a continuous, self-reinforcing process - and each cycle builds on what the previous one learned.

## 5. How we work with leaders and teams at this threshold

### Co-creation of the desired culture shift

Rather than defining the new culture at the top and rolling it out downward, we bring a representative group from across the organisation (including the senior leadership team) into the process. When people help build it, they become its ambassadors - not because they were told to be, but because they were part of it.

### Leadership team coaching

- The CEO and top team must go first - not as sponsors of a programme, but as genuine participants in the same journey they are asking of others
- We start by working with the leadership team to define: What is the culture we want to create?
- We then do extended 1:1 coaching with each leadership team member (can be supported by LCP) - how am I creating the current state; what shift is required in me for the future state?
- Note: Step 1 is always regulation: Before any cultural aspiration can take root, people need to be able to get present. A nervous system in chronic stress cannot create, connect or transform. Teaching leaders to self-regulate - and to create the conditions for others to do the same - is foundational

### Whole-system engagement

We work with cultural ambassadors to spread this new culture through a "wave" model: transformation spreads not through a single cascade but through warming, inspiring and involving - waves that go out and return, each carrying something back.

We support the organisation in setting up structures including:
- Cultural workshops in different locations / departments to create containers for honest dialogue about culture change
- Feedback loops to feed back reflections and ideas
- Peer coaching and peer learning so that self-examination becomes habitual rather than occasional

The Luminous distinction: we are not here to install processes. We are here to help people see themselves and each other differently - with unconditional positive regard, genuine curiosity, and a willingness to meet what is real. That is what changes cultures.

## 6. Additional reflections about this threshold

- **The Luminous distinction is the depth of the people work:** many organisations can offer culture change programmes. What is different here is the willingness to go to the real thing - not the surface behaviours, but the fears, assumptions and patterns underneath. That is what creates a lasting shift
- **Vulnerability is the turning point:** Susan's story of a frustrated leader who finally broke down and shared how she truly felt - and how that shifted the whole room - captures something essential. The moment of honest exposure is often when transformation actually begins
- **The CEO's readiness shapes everything:** an organisation can only transform to the level its leader is willing to go. If the leader is performing openness rather than embodying it, people know. This is the single most important factor in whether this work succeeds
- **This threshold may be harder to position externally:** unlike career transitions or scale, "organisational transformation" is a crowded space with lots of established players. The conversation raised a genuine question about how we distinguish ourselves - the answer seems to lie not in what we do, but in how we do it and how deep we are willing to go
