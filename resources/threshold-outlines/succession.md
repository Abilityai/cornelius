## 1. Description of this threshold

- This threshold arises when a family business transitions leadership from one generation to the next - and the work is rarely just about handing over a title
- At its heart, it is a tension between old and new: honouring the legacy of the founders while creating space for the next generation to lead in their own right
- The incoming generation must find their own leadership identity - not just manage what was built, but actively choose what they want to carry forward and what they want to leave behind
- Unlike corporate succession, this cannot be solved through org design alone: it is relational, systemic, and often emotionally charged
- Note: for now, we have decided to keep the focus on family businesses - resist blending with generic corporate succession, which has a different texture and would dilute the message

## 2. Typical client profile

### Lead client:
- Next-generation family business leaders, typically in their 30s-40s, who are expected (explicitly or implicitly) to take over
- Often highly educated - frequently at world-class institutions - in contrast to parents who may have built from little formal education
- Have grown up with the business as part of their identity, sometimes without being given a real choice about it
- The founding generation (typically the father) is still alive and often present - sometimes officially, sometimes from the sidelines

### Additional involved stakeholders:
- Previous generation who is "passing the torch" - often have built the business themselves; have a difficult time letting go
- Additional family members; senior executives who are not family members but directly involved with running the business
- Businesses range from mid-size independents to large multi-generational enterprises; may include cousins, siblings and extended family as co-owners or stakeholders

## 3. Common signs / challenges at this threshold

- The founder won't fully let go - may have stepped back in name but continues to control decisions, making it hard for the next gen to truly lead
- Identity confusion - next-gen leaders have often been shaped around the business; they may not know who they are separate from it, or whether they even want to carry it forward
- Obligation vs. choice - some heirs don't actually want to take over but feel they have no option; others want it but are blocked
- Inter- and intra-generational conflict - sibling rivalry, unholy alliances, sidelining of weaker performers, competing claims on power
- Strategic disagreements - the new generation wants to innovate or sell; the parents want to protect the family name and stay conservative
- Governance gaps - decision-making is murky; you can't simply elect one CEO when multiple cousins or siblings are involved
- Trust barriers - family businesses are often reluctant to open their books or let outsiders in, making it hard to get real help
- Transference of parental fears - even very wealthy next-gen leaders can carry a deep, irrational fear of losing everything, absorbed from parents who built from nothing

## 4. The opportunity for growth

- **For the next-gen leader:** the chance to actively choose their own path - to lead from purpose rather than obligation, and ask for the first time: what do I actually want?
- **Shift from status to impact:** next-gen leaders are consistently moving away from their parents' motivation (survival, building a name) toward something more: long-term impact, stewardship, contribution to society
- **For the family system:** the opportunity to replace unspoken alliances and avoidance with genuine alignment - a shared vision, clearer governance, and healthier relationships
- **For the business:** succession done well builds longevity; handled badly, it quietly destroys what took generations to create

## 5. How we work with leaders and families at this threshold

### Individual coaching

Deep 1:1 work with the next-gen leader, including:
- Clarifying their own identity, values and purpose, separate from the family business
- Exploring what they genuinely want to carry forward vs. leave behind
- Working through transference, inherited fears and the weight of expectation

### Family systems / Systemic work

Because this threshold can't be resolved at the individual level alone. We work with practitioners experienced in family systems and constellations work to:
- Surface what is really going on beneath the surface (alliances, resentments, unspoken dynamics) and understand and shift systemic patterns
- Help the family align on a shared long-term vision for the business
- Clarify roles, decision-making authority and governance
- Support the founding generation to genuinely transition out, not just nominally step back

### Retreat / cohort offering

An immersive peer experience for next-gen family business leaders to share experiences, exchange notes and reduce the isolation of this transition:
- Combines personal development work (values, identity, vision) with peer learning
- A natural entry point to deeper individual or family work

## 6. Additional reflections about this threshold

- **It's a systemic issue:** Even excellent individual coaching has limited impact if the family system isn't addressed - the real breakthroughs come when we can work with enough of the whole
- **The founding generation is often the harder conversation:** Next-gen leaders are typically hungry to grow and open to new thinking; founders are not always willing - but their willingness (or lack of it) shapes everything
- **Next-gen family business leaders show remarkable potential:** Seeing how far next-gen leaders have already shifted in their motivations - away from money and status, toward impact, meaning, even spirituality. The potential in this group is remarkable
